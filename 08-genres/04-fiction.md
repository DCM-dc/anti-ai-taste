# 08-genres / 04 · Fiction

## Scope

Short stories, novels, narrative prose, literary fiction, genre fiction
(sci-fi, fantasy, mystery, romance).

This genre **relaxes several universal rules** and adds genre-specific
requirements (sensory detail, voice, dialogue, scene vs summary).

---

## Universal rules that apply directly

- **Rhetoric:** no forced elevation at the end (the story should end
  where the story ends, not with a moral).
- **Rhetoric:** no "Key Takeaways" list (fiction doesn't summarize).
- **Language:** signature-word density ≤ 3 per 500 words (fiction
  should be near 0 — signature words break voice).

---

## Universal rules that RELAX for fiction

### Relaxation 1: Coining terms is allowed (for genre fiction)

The universal rule says "don't coin terms." For sci-fi and fantasy,
**coinage is often required** — the world has things our world doesn't.

```
✓ "The ansible flickered."  ← coined term (Le Guin); genre-appropriate
✓ "He checked his comm-link."  ← coined; genre convention
✗ "The Ansible of Convergence"  ← coined term dressed as deep concept
```

The rule: **coin for objects in the world, not for concepts about the
world.** "Ansible" (a thing that exists in the story) is fine.
"Ansible's Law" (a pseudo-deep framing) is not.

### Relaxation 2: Voice can be extreme

The universal rule says "register should vary but stay grounded." For
fiction, **voice can be extreme** — first person unreliable, dialect,
stream of consciousness.

```
✓ "I ain't seen nothing. You hear me? Nothing."  ← dialect voice
✓ "The — the thing — it was there, in the corner, and I —"  ← stream of consciousness
```

The rule: **voice must be consistent within a character**. Don't switch
dialects mid-scene unless the character is switching registers.

### Relaxation 3: Fragments and run-ons are tools

The universal rhythm rule says "use fragments sparingly." For fiction,
**fragments and run-ons are legitimate rhythmic tools**.

```
✓ "She ran. Door. Keys. Where were the keys."  ← panic, fragments
✓ "and he kept talking and talking and the words ran into each other
   like they were all one word and she couldn't breathe"  ← run-on, anxiety
```

The rule: **rhythm must match the moment**. Fragments for panic,
run-ons for overwhelm, complete sentences for calm.

## Genre-specific requirements

### Requirement 1: Sensory detail

Fiction requires concrete sensory detail. Not "it was a nice day" —
specific senses.

```
✗ "The room was pleasant."  ← abstract
✓ "The room smelled like old paper and lemon oil. Dust motes turned
   in the afternoon light."  ← olfactory + visual, specific
```

The universal rule "every claim must be grounded" applies here as
"every scene must be embodied." Abstract prose is the AI tell in fiction.

### Requirement 2: Dialogue has subtext

```
✗ "I'm angry," she said angrily.  ← telling, no subtext
✓ "Fine," she said. She set the cup down harder than she needed to.
   ← showing, subtext in action
```

AI-generated dialogue often states the emotion directly ("she said
angrily"). Real dialogue shows the emotion through action, pause,
and what's NOT said.

### Requirement 3: Scene vs summary

Fiction alternates between **scene** (moment-by-moment, with dialogue
and sensory detail) and **summary** (time compressed, "the next three
weeks passed").

AI fiction often defaults to **all summary** — events described but
not lived. Real fiction spends most of its word count in scene.

```
✗ (all summary) "They fought and then made up and then went home."
✓ (scene) "The keys hit the counter. 'You always do this,' she said.
   He didn't look up. The refrigerator hummed. 'Do what?' 'This.'
   She waved at nothing. The kitchen felt smaller than it had five
   minutes ago."
```

### Requirement 4: Characters have specific desire and obstacle

Every scene needs a character who wants something specific and faces
a specific obstacle. Vague wanting produces vague scenes.

```
✗ "She wanted to be happy."  ← vague
✓ "She wanted the promotion, and she wanted it before Patel got it."
   ← specific desire, specific rival
```

### Requirement 5: Endings are earned, not elevated

The universal "end where the thought ends" applies. For fiction, this
means: **end where the story's tension resolves**, not with a moral.

```
✗ "And she realized that love was the answer all along."  ← forced elevation
✓ "She locked the door and didn't look back. The bus came at 6:14.
   She was on it."  ← specific ending, no moralizing
```

## Genre-specific smells

### Smell 1: The "comprehensive emotions" tell

```
✗ "She felt a wave of emotions wash over her — sadness, anger,
   regret, and a fleeting sense of hope."
```

Listing emotions is the AI tell for "I don't know how to render a
specific feeling." Real fiction picks one or two and embodies them.

### Smell 2: The "comprehensive description" tell

```
✗ "The old house stood on the hill, its weathered wooden exterior
   telling stories of bygone eras, surrounded by lush greenery that
   whispered secrets to the wind."
```

Adjective-stacking + personification + signature words ("bygone eras,"
"whispered secrets"). Real description picks specific details.

### Smell 3: The "perfectly balanced" dialogue

```
✗ "I understand your perspective," he said calmly.
   "And I appreciate yours," she replied thoughtfully.
```

Real dialogue is messier — interruptions, half-thoughts, people not
listening. AI dialogue is too balanced, too complete.

### Smell 4: The omniscient explanatory narrator

```
✗ "Little did she know, this would change everything."
✗ "What she didn't realize was that the answer had been right in
   front of her all along."
```

These are AI reflexes for foreshadowing. Real foreshadowing is
indirect — a detail planted that pays off later, not a narrator
telling us something big is coming.

## Instruction template (fiction-specific)

```
Apply universal rules, plus these RELAXATIONS:

COINING: allowed for objects in the world (ansible, comm-link),
         not for concepts about the world (Ansible's Law).
VOICE: can be extreme (dialect, stream of consciousness), but
       consistent within a character.
RHYTHM: fragments and run-ons are tools. Match rhythm to moment
        (panic = fragments, overwhelm = run-on, calm = complete).

GENRE REQUIREMENTS:
- Sensory detail: every scene embodied in specific senses
- Dialogue: subtext through action, not stated emotion
- Scene vs summary: spend most word count in scene
- Characters: specific desire + specific obstacle
- Endings: earned by story's tension resolving, not elevated

GENRE SMELLS:
- No "wave of emotions" lists
- No adjective-stacking + personification in description
- No perfectly balanced dialogue
- No omniscient foreshadowing ("little did she know")
```
